Full technical plan: docs/plan-bot-v1.md · Built from 6 research agents + 3 adversarial reviewers · 2026-07-15

1 · The big picture

What we are building: a bot that decides once a day whether to hold BTC/ETH or stay in cash, executes those decisions automatically in a simulated (paper) account against real market prices, records everything it does in a permanent memory, and shows you the results in a private web dashboard on your PC. You never touch code. Its trading decisions never need the GPU; the local AI runs only while your manual switch is on (ai on / ai off — it can never start itself in the middle of a game), and it needs zero real API keys while in paper mode.

What we are NOT building (yet): anything that trades real money, anything intraday, shorts/leverage, stock markets, or a bot that "rewires itself" from its own trades. Those are explicitly out of v1.

The video (Miles Deutscher)

His one genuinely good idea is the one you spotted: bots need memory. But his implementation — an LLM writing itself a "lesson" after every trade and obeying it — is textbook overfitting: one trade proves nothing (statistically, even a full year of daily trades can't distinguish a good strategy from a coin flip). We adopt the idea the rigorous way: memory informs, only validated experiments authorize changes. Rejected: LLM as the live decision brain, casual "what if RSI…" backtesting with no experiment registry, and the $8K/week claims (unverifiable marketing).

2 · The three iron rules

  1. No time travel. The bot can only use information that already existed at decision time — enforced by an automatic check on every single decision, plus a clock-integrity check against Binance's server time.
  2. Every experiment is registered before it runs. Every strategy variant, every threshold, every future challenger, and the paper test itself. This is what makes the statistics honest (Deflated Sharpe Ratio).
  3. Memory informs; only the registry authorizes. The journal generates ideas and monitors health. Nothing in production changes except through a registered, validated experiment. The bot never modifies itself.

3 · What the bot actually trades

96-hour horizon only. The 24h horizon was cut by our own math: each round trip costs ~0.30–0.40% in fees+slippage, and at daily-trading frequency that friction eats more than the entire measurable edge. At 96h it's survivable.

StrategyWhat it doesStatus

Position sizes are set by volatility targeting (12%/year target): when markets get wild, positions shrink automatically. Typical exposure will be 20–40% of capital — that is correct behavior, not timidity.

4 · The memory (your pillar)

Every day the bot writes to an append-only journal — nothing is ever edited or deleted, corrections are new entries (like an accountant's ledger):

  • Every decision, including "do nothing" — with the full snapshot of data the bot saw, the model version, the config fingerprint, and a machine-readable reason ("probability below threshold", "regime gate red", "stale data — held").
  • Every simulated order and fill — with the fees and slippage charged.
  • Daily equity, positions, and health metrics — data drift, calibration, PnL control charts.
  • Every human intervention, missed cycle, and alert — first-class events.

The safe learning loop: the journal can quickly teach us about costs, bugs and calibration — but with ~10–20 trades per 6 months it can never rank strategies (the math is unforgiving). So ideas born from the journal become hypotheses, hypotheses become registered experiments on 6 years of history in the lab, and only winners get promoted. The bot itself stays frozen during the whole test — no monthly retraining, no self-adjustment.

Durability: nightly export + encrypted cloud backup (password escrowed outside the machine) + quarterly restore drill on a separate target. The journal is designed to outlive the PC, the code, and DuckDB itself.

5 · Safety rails (what protects the account)

Hard kill−20% drawdown → sell everything, halt, page you. Restart needs a written manual review. Soft de-risk−10% drawdown → position sizes cut in half automatically. Daily loss limits−2.5% in a day → no new entries for 48h. −5% → treated as a kill event. Stale dataIf any data feed is older than its expected publication schedule → bot holds and alerts. Blind for 3+ days → moves to cash and halts. It never trades on a guess. Order sanityMax order size, price bands, duplicate-order protection, daily ledger reconciliation, max 8 orders/day. Dead-man's switchThe bot pings an external service on every successful cycle; if the pings stop (PC off, asleep, crashed), the external service alerts your Telegram. Silence is always detectable. Your remote controlTelegram commands — /pause /flatten /resume /status — accepted only from your Telegram user ID; /flatten works even if the model is broken.

6 · The phases

B0

Lab prerequisites · small

Three small changes to CryptoAcademy so its good parts can be packaged as a frozen library ("wheel") the bot installs: path override, GPU dependencies split out (the bot's environment won't even contain torch), and a data-schema contract so the bot can detect if a data source silently changes format years from now.

B1

Clean repo skeleton · C:\CryptoBot

New private GitHub repo with the bootstrap script (one command sets up any Windows PC: environment, pinned dependencies, secrets into Credential Manager, journal DB, historical data re-download, scheduled tasks), the journal schema, CI, secret-leak scanning, and the core safety tests.

B2

Strategy research · in the lab, GPU-free

Implement S1–S3, register every configuration first (~8–12), evaluate exactly once with purged cross-validation, net of conservative costs, on data up to 2026-07-11. The combination rule is frozen before seeing any results (choosing weights after seeing results is a hidden, unregistered experiment — the reviewers caught this). Output: one frozen champion configuration, honestly written up.

B3

The engine

One daily process: fetch data → validate → decide → simulate fills → journal, all in a single transaction so a crash mid-cycle can never corrupt the record (there's a test that literally kills the process halfway and checks recovery). Conservative fill simulation: full fees, doubled slippage in wild markets, never same-bar execution — paper results are never allowed to flatter.

B4

Web UI + operations

Your local dashboard (NiceGUI, only reachable from your own PC, session-token protected): positions, today's decision and its reasons in plain English, equity vs benchmarks, health panel, pause/flatten buttons. Telegram digests and pages. Scheduled tasks. At the end of B4 the whole configuration is hash-frozen — from then on only "plumbing" fixes are allowed; any change to the decision logic restarts the test clock.

B5

Burn-in · ~1 month

The bot runs live-paper at size zero: journals everything, trades nothing. Purpose: shake out plumbing failures (missed cycles, feed hiccups, alert storms) before the real test clock starts. Exit: two consecutive clean weeks.

B6

The paper test · 6–18 months, date fixed in advance

The acceptance rules — including the exact evaluation date, computed from the champion's real trade frequency — are registered before day one and can't be moved (ending a test "when the curve looks good" is a classic self-deception the reviewers flagged). The bot runs hands-off. Benchmarks run as shadow strategies inside the same engine, so nobody can recompute them favorably later.

B7

Verdict & decision

PASS
Zero unhandled incidents, paper matches the simulation, not inferior to the no-signal benchmark. Means: "plumbing certified + non-inferior" — explicitly not proof of skill (that takes years). You decide what's next.
INCONCLUSIVE
Too few trades (e.g., the bot was correctly flat through a bear market). Rails certified; entry quality untested. This branch existing at all is a reviewer fix — the most likely outcome must have a pre-registered answer.
FAIL / STOP
Kill switch fired, negative net Sharpe, or costs ate >50% of gross profit. Published post-mortem; redesign goes back through the registry. An honest failure is a valid result.

7 · Honest expectations

  • The measured edge is small (MCC 0.093 at 96h). Sizing assumes a net Sharpe of 0.2–0.5 — not the numbers influencers quote.
  • >80% of measured retail traders lose money net of costs. The realistic win for this bot is losing much less in bear markets than buy-and-hold, not out-earning a bull market.
  • 6–18 months of paper trading cannot mathematically prove a small edge — the test certifies that the machine works and isn't worse than a mechanical benchmark. Anything claiming more would be v2's sin again.
  • A no-skill bot would pass some gates by luck — so we compute and publish that false-pass probability next to the verdict.
  • Any result showing net Sharpe > 2 gets treated as a bug until proven otherwise.
